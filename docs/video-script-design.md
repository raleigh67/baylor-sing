# Video Script — Visual Design & Editorial Direction

**Estimated runtime:** ~3:30 spoken
**Tone:** Conversational, confident. Read it like a director walking someone through a show, not like a presentation deck.
**Stage directions** are in *italics* — cue the editor to cut to the relevant screen.

---

## Open — the framing

> Baylor Sing is a show. Sixty-seven acts over four years, each one a tiny musical with costumes and lights and a backdrop and a story. So when we set out to build a website about it, we didn't want it to feel like a research paper. We wanted it to feel like a night at the show.

*Cut to: the curtain-rise hero animation. Gold "Sing" wordmark fades in as the red velvet curtains pull apart.*

> That's the whole organizing idea. The site opens with curtains. It closes with curtains. And in between, it walks you through the cast, the music, the color, and the verdict — just like an actual evening of Sing would.

---

## The visual language

> The design is built around two materials. Red velvet — for the theatrical moments. And gold leaf — for the moments that need to feel earned. The font is Playfair Display in italic, set big and a little bit dramatic, because that's what the wordmark on the original Sing logo does.

*Cut to: side-by-side of the gold cursive Sing logo + a sample of the Playfair italic headings.*

> Then on the analytical pages — the parts where you're actually reading charts — we shift into a darker, quieter register. Black backgrounds. Hairline gold accents. The charts themselves use color, but it's the *acts' own* color. Every dot you see on the audio map is filled with the actual dominant color from that act's costumes and stage. The data paints itself.

*Cut to: audio map close-up, hovering one dot to show its palette swatches in the tooltip.*

---

## The arc — seven scenes

> The site has seven scenes, and each one has a job.

*Cut to: scroll through the page slowly, holding on each scene as it's named.*

> **Scene one** is the curtain rise. No data. Just mood. Gold logo, red velvet, a scroll cue. We're earning the right to ask the reader to keep going.

> **Scene two** — the cast. Sixty-seven palette tiles, one per act. Hovering tells you the group and theme. Clicking one filters the entire rest of the site to that act. We wanted the reader to feel like they're picking a face out of the program before the show starts.

> **Scene three** is the sound. Four charts. The audio map plots every act in valence-by-energy space — that's basically *how happy* versus *how intense*. Then the most-covered artists across four years. Then how old the songs are. Then the genre mix.

> **Scene four** is the color. The biggest analytical opportunity in the project. Four charts again — the years stacked as palettes, a color wheel, a saturation-by-brightness scatter, and four extremes. Most vivid act, most muted, brightest, darkest.

> **Scene five** is the verdict. Theatrical mode comes back. Pigskin reveal — the gold medals. First-place spotlights, one per year.

> **Scene six** is *behind the curtain*. Two short paragraphs explaining how the data was actually made. Quick methodology note.

> **Scene seven** is the encore. Ten fun-fact cards. The closing-credits energy.

---

## The interaction

> The thing we're most proud of is the per-act focus. You click any tile, any dot, any palette strip — and a sticky bar slides down from the top of the screen saying "Focused on Kappa Omega Tau 2022 — It's a Gnome's World." Every chart on the page dims everything that isn't that act. The audio map highlights that one dot. The jukebox switches from the top-20 to that act's actual song list. The genre chart switches from four stacked years to a breakdown of that one act's tags. The song age switches from 432 songs to that act's twelve.

> One click. Whole site recontextualizes around a single act. One more click on "show all" — back to the aggregate.

*Cut to: live demo. Click a cast tile. Show all the charts dimming and refocusing.*

---

## Why theatrical, not editorial-clean

> We could have built a clean modern data journalism site. NYT-style. White background, serif headlines, lots of breathing room. And it would have been correct. But it would have missed the point. Sing isn't a quiet thing — it's a loud thing. It's a thousand people in sequins on a stage with a brass section and a fog machine. The website needed to have a temperature. The red velvet is the temperature.

---

## Close

> Every design decision came back to one question: would this feel like the show? When the answer was yes, we kept it. When the answer was no, we cut it. That's how we ended up here.

*Cut to: pull back to the encore screen. Hold on the gold "Sing" credits.*
