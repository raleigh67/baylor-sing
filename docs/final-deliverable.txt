===== DESIGN PROCESS =====

The project started from a single observation: Baylor Sing is a show, not a
research paper. Sixty-seven acts, each a self-contained musical with
costumes, lights, a painted backdrop, and a thematic premise. The earliest
iteration of the website looked like a generic dashboard, and it felt wrong
the moment we saw it. So we restarted with a new organizing question:
"What if the website itself were staged like a Sing performance?"

That question dictated nearly everything that followed. The site opens with
red velvet curtains pulling aside to reveal a gold cursive "Sing" wordmark,
the same way the actual show opens at Waco Hall. It closes the same way —
gold credits, a final reveal of the year's winners. Between those two
moments, the reader walks through seven "scenes": a curtain rise (intro),
the cast (an overview of every act), the sound (audio analysis), the color
(palette analysis), the verdict (placement reveals), a methodology note,
and an encore of fun facts.

The two tonal registers — theatrical bookends, analytical middle — were a
deliberate choice. Pure theatrical maximalism throughout would have made
the data hard to read; pure editorial cleanness would have stripped out the
emotional reason the project exists. The pattern lets each register serve
its purpose. When the reader is in the data, the visuals are restrained
(dark backgrounds, hairline gold accents, charts colored by the acts' own
palettes). When the reader is in a transition or a reveal, the red velvet
and gold filigree come back.

The most distinctive interaction is per-act focus. Clicking any cast tile,
any chart dot, any palette strip, or any verdict tile triggers a sticky bar
at the top of the page ("Focused on Kappa Omega Tau 2022 — It's a Gnome's
World") and reframes every chart on the page to highlight or filter to
that single act. The audio map dims down to one dot. The jukebox switches
from the top-20 artists across all acts to that act's actual song list with
artists. The song-age histogram redraws using just that act's songs. The
genre chart switches from year-by-year stacked bars to a horizontal
breakdown of that act's specific genre tags. Clicking "× show all"
reverses everything in one move. The mechanism is a single event bus —
charts register a highlight() callback at render time and the orchestrator
broadcasts to all of them.

The data layer is built once at site-build time into a single JSON file
(67 act objects, ~58 KB). All derived fields — dominant color, weighted-
average HSV, per-song ages, palette source — are pre-computed in Python so
the frontend does no math beyond layout. This kept the JavaScript focused
on rendering and the code small enough that no framework was needed:
vanilla JS, hand-rolled SVG, ~10 chart modules, total under 2,000 lines.


===== DATA MAPPING — WHY EACH FEATURE GOES WHERE =====

Audio Map (valence × energy scatter):
- X axis: valence (Spotify's musical-positivity score, 0–1). Sad acts
  toward the left, happy toward the right. This mirrors the conventional
  left/right reading of "negative/positive" in Western data viz.
- Y axis: energy (Spotify's intensity score, 0–1). Mellow at the bottom,
  frenetic at the top — vertical position intuitively maps to "high/low."
- Dot fill: the act's vivid pick — the most saturated × bright color in
  the act's palette, weighted slightly by proportion. We chose this over
  "first dominant" because pure-dominant often lands on near-black
  background pixels; vivid-pick gives the color a viewer would actually
  remember.
- Dot size: medal acts (1st/2nd/3rd) drawn larger than Pigskin top-8,
  which are drawn larger than Participated. Importance encoded by area.
- Gold double ring: medal-placement acts. Gold is the universal medal
  signifier and pairs with the site's color system.
- Bronze ring: Pigskin top-8 (non-medal) — warmer than the muted base
  but cooler than the gold, preserving the placement hierarchy visually.

Color Wheel (polar scatter):
- Angle: dominant hue, 0° at top (red) and rotating clockwise through
  yellow, green, cyan, blue, magenta. Matches a canonical color wheel so
  the chart is readable by anyone who's seen one.
- Radius: dominant saturation. Center = grey/muted, rim = vivid. This is
  the standard polar HSV representation.
- Dot fill: the act's own dominant color. Every dot is literally painted
  in the data it represents — the chart colors itself.
- Zoom controls: click "Warm" / "Cool" / "Purple" / "Green" to zoom into
  a hue quadrant. Lets the viewer compare acts within a color family
  without losing the visual context.

Saturation × Brightness scatter:
- X: average palette saturation. Y: average palette brightness. Both
  weighted across the palette, ignoring near-black pixels.
- Quadrant labels: "vivid + bright" (top-right) = punch-you-in-the-face
  acts. "Muted + dark" (bottom-left) = moody. The four quadrants are
  semantically meaningful, so zoom controls let the viewer focus.
- Dot fill: vivid pick (same convention as the audio map and color
  wheel, for cross-chart visual consistency).

Years in Color (stacked palette strips):
- Four horizontal strips, one per year. Each strip contains every act
  from that year laid out chronologically, with the act's full 10-color
  palette stacked vertically within its slot. Tile width is equal across
  acts, so the year's chromatic identity reads as a whole — does 2024
  lean warmer than 2022? — without distortion from any one big act.

The Extremes (4 callout cards):
- Most vivid act (highest saturation × brightness in dominant color).
- Most muted act (lowest weighted-average saturation).
- Brightest act (highest weighted-average brightness).
- Darkest act (lowest weighted-average brightness).
- Big swatch + group + theme on each card. The point isn't the numbers;
  it's the visual extremes the dataset contains.

Sing Jukebox (top-20 artists):
- Horizontal bar chart, ranked by # of acts that covered each artist.
- Horizontal because artist names are long; horizontal handles long
  labels better than vertical bars.
- Gold bars throughout — single hue keeps the focus on the magnitude
  comparison, not on category encoding.

Song Age histogram:
- X: years between a song's release date (from Spotify enrichment) and
  the year of the Sing performance.
- 7 buckets: 0-4, 5-9, 10-14, 15-19, 20-29, 30-49, 50+.
- Bucket widths chosen non-uniformly to reflect the long tail — most
  songs cluster in the 5-15 year range, but older songs deserve their
  own bins to make the leap into the '70s and '80s visible.

Genre Proportions:
- Stacked vertical bars, one per year. Top 8 genres globally.
- Stacked because the question is "what's the genre mix?" — a
  composition question, which stacks answer well. Each genre gets a
  distinct color in the theatrical palette (gold, wine red, emerald,
  plum, slate, mauve, peach, cream) so the legend reads cleanly.

The Verdict (Pigskin reveal + first-place spotlights):
- 33 placement tiles (12 medals + 21 Pigskin non-medal). Medal tiles
  get a single gold outline; 1st-place tiles get a thicker double-gold
  treatment. Visual hierarchy = placement hierarchy.
- 4 first-place spotlight cards (one per year), each showing the year,
  group, theme in italics, and the act's palette strip.

Encore (10 fun-fact cards):
- Big-number callouts in gold gradient Playfair italic for findings
  like average valence, oldest cover, fastest tempo, average song age.
- One hero card for the headline fact (avg valence percentage) spans
  the full grid width. Sub-cards are 3-up on desktop, collapsing to
  2-up and 1-up at smaller breakpoints.


===== KNOWN COMPROMISES =====

- 5 acts (of 67) lack a usable YouTube source video, so their palettes
  fall back to scraped photos and are flagged with a "no video" badge
  in the Cast scene and excluded from aggregate color charts.
- 2 acts lack Spotify audio features and are listed as a footnote
  under the Audio Map rather than plotted.


===== TECH STACK =====

Python 3.12 + pandas (data assembly), vanilla JavaScript + hand-rolled
SVG (frontend), scrollama (scroll-triggered scene tracking), yt-dlp
(YouTube frame extraction), scikit-learn KMeans (palette clustering),
Pillow (image handling), pytest (data-builder tests). No frontend
framework. No build step. Deploys as static files.
